Youth Without Borders, Cinema as Connection — Cogdel Student Film Project

March 27, 2026 | Cogdel School Chengdu

What happens when you give young filmmakers a camera and a cross-cultural challenge? "Youth Without Borders: Cinema as Connection" — Cogdel School Chengdu's latest student-led initiative — answered that question with extraordinary results.

The Project

Over two months, Cogdel students from Grades 7–11 collaborated with peers from partner schools internationally to produce short films exploring the theme of "Home" — what it means, how it looks, and how it feels across different cultures. Working entirely in English, students handled every stage of production: concept development, scriptwriting, location scouting, cinematography, editing, sound design, and final presentation.

This is project-based learning at its finest — combining visual literacy, cross-cultural communication, storytelling, technical production, and collaborative project management into a single, authentic challenge.

The Films

Five short films premiered at the campus screening, ranging from documentary to stop-motion animation:

  • A poignant documentary on Anren's 80-year architectural heritage, narrated by a student whose grandparents also studied in these buildings
  • A stop-motion animation tracing a student's emotional journey between two cultures and two languages
  • An experimental film juxtaposing daily life in Chengdu with that of partner-school students abroad — same time, different worlds
  • A narrative short about a boarding student discovering that "home" isn't only where you come from, but where you are truly seen
  • A documentary following Cogdel's campus wildlife — the squirrels and peacocks that have become unofficial school mascots

Principal Lu Kun's Reflection

"Education isn't just about sending children to university. It's about ensuring that when they face the world, they are thoughtful, warm, courageous individuals with their own voice. The Panda Vision Film Festival — and projects like this — are about teaching students to express themselves, not just to take exams."

Why Cinema?

Film is uniquely suited to developing multiple dimensions of competency simultaneously. It demands visual literacy, narrative thinking, technical skill, and — crucially — perspective-taking. A filmmaker must constantly ask: how will this image be understood by someone who doesn't share my context? That question lies at the heart of cross-cultural competence.

The films are now being submitted to international youth film festivals. A public screening for the Chengdu international community is being planned.
